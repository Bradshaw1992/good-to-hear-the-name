# v1.6 Player Bios

52 bios (53 players minus 2 scrapped — Antony, Arnautović). Goes in the `story` field of each player record in content.json. ~400-600 chars each.

## Daniele De Rossi
Roma to the bone. De Rossi spent 18 years in the Giallorossi shirt, a snarling, cultured midfielder who could thread a pass and break up play with equal venom. He won the 2006 World Cup with Italy at 22, despite a four-match ban for elbowing McBride, and went on to captain his country. A Coppa Italia and two Italian Supercups were all Roma could give him, but his loyalty became legend. A late-career sabbatical at Boca Juniors fulfilled a boyhood dream, and a brief, doomed spell as Roma manager closed the loop on a one-man civic institution.

## Steven Nzonzi
The languid Frenchman with the Afro who walked into Sevilla and walked out a Europa League winner and a World Cup champion. Nzonzi started his English life at Blackburn and Stoke, where his composure on the ball stood out in a side built for chaos. Sevilla turned him into a genuinely elite holding midfielder, and Didier Deschamps rewarded him with a spot in France's 2018 squad, where he came on in the final against Croatia. Roma, Galatasaray and Rennes followed, but Russia 2018 is the line on his CV that does the talking.

## Sami Khedira
The engine of two giants. Khedira broke through at Stuttgart, winning the Bundesliga in 2007, then spent five years at Real Madrid where he was a key part of La Decima in 2014. Weeks later he lifted the World Cup with Germany in the Maracana, the perfect summer. Juventus snapped him up on a free and he won four straight Scudetti in Turin, the metronome alongside Pirlo and later Pjanic. Injuries chipped away at the back end, but a brief Hertha Berlin send-off closed a career most midfielders would trade their boots for.

## Mats Hummels
The thinking man's centre-back. Hummels came through Bayern's academy but made his name at Dortmund, where he was central to Klopp's back-to-back Bundesliga titles and the 2013 Champions League final run. His header won Germany the 2014 World Cup quarter-final against France, on the way to lifting the trophy in Brazil. A move home to Bayern brought three more league titles before he returned to Dortmund. Famously left out of Low's 2018 World Cup squad, he had the last laugh by clambering back into the side for Euro 2020.

## Luca Toni
A late bloomer who never stopped scoring. Toni bounced around the Italian lower leagues into his late twenties before exploding at Palermo and then Fiorentina, where his 31 league goals in 2005-06 won him the European Golden Shoe. Months later he was a World Cup winner with Italy. Bayern Munich came calling and he delivered a Bundesliga and DFB-Pokal double in his first season. The fairy-tale ending arrived at Verona, where at 37 he won the Capocannoniere, the oldest top scorer in Serie A history, complete with his trademark ear-twist celebration.

## Matt Le Tissier
Le God. The most gifted English footballer of his generation never played for a top-six club, refusing every approach to stay at Southampton, where he scored 209 goals from midfield in 16 years. He converted 47 of 48 penalties, a record that will never be broken, and produced a highlight reel of volleys, lobs and dribbles that still feels unreal. England capped him eight times, a national scandal. A divisive post-football turn into conspiracy theories has soured his public image, but on the pitch he was pure magic.

## Robbie Keane
Ireland's all-time top scorer with 68 goals and a career that read like a tour of European football. Keane was a teenage sensation at Wolves, then bounced through Coventry, an unhappy Inter spell and Leeds before finding his home at Tottenham, where he formed a brilliant partnership with Berbatov. A short, awkward Liverpool stint followed, then a loan back to Villa and the move that defined his late career: LA Galaxy, where he won two MLS Cups alongside Beckham and Donovan. The somersault celebration was the constant across all of it.

## Dwight Yorke
That smile. Yorke was the laid-back genius of Manchester United's 1999 Treble, forming a telepathic partnership with Andy Cole that fired them to the Premier League, FA Cup and Champions League in one of football's great seasons. He had emerged at Aston Villa under Graham Taylor and Brian Little, where his pace and finishing made him a Villa Park favourite. Blackburn, Birmingham, Sunderland and a fondly remembered spell at Sydney FC followed, where he was the marquee signing of the inaugural A-League. Forever Trinidad and Tobago's most famous footballer.

## Dimitar Berbatov
Languid, brilliant, and seemingly above it all. Berbatov made his name at Bayer Leverkusen, including a run to the 2002 Champions League final, before Tottenham brought him to the Premier League. Two ludicrously good seasons in north London earned a club-record move to Manchester United, where he won two titles and shared the Golden Boot in 2010-11 with a five-goal haul against Blackburn. Fulham, Monaco, Greek football and even a stop in India followed. Bulgaria's all-time top scorer, with the touch of a man who had nothing to prove.

## Park Ji-sung
The lung-busting heartbeat of a great Manchester United side. Park caught the world's eye at the 2002 World Cup under Guus Hiddink, followed him to PSV and then to Old Trafford in 2005, where Sir Alex used him in the biggest games against the best opposition. He won three Premier League titles and started the 2008 Champions League final on the bench, but he made history by lifting the trophy the next year. South Korea's captain at the 2010 World Cup, and the first Asian player to win the Champions League. Loved everywhere he played.

## Nwankwo Kanu
A 6'5" magician with feet of silk. Kanu won the Champions League with Ajax at 19, then heart surgery at Inter almost ended everything. Arsenal took the gamble, and he repaid them by scoring a 15-minute hat-trick at Chelsea and becoming a key squad member in the Invincibles season. Portsmouth was his late-career home, where he scored the winner in the 2008 FA Cup final. Add an Olympic gold with Nigeria in 1996 and two African Footballer of the Year awards and you have one of the most decorated Africans ever to play the game.

## Adriano
L'Imperatore. For a few years in the mid-2000s Adriano was the most physically terrifying striker on the planet, a Brazilian battering ram with a left foot like a cannon who fired Inter to back-to-back Serie A titles. His Copa America in 2004 was one of the great individual tournaments. Then his father died, and the joy drained out of his football. He drifted home to Brazil, where a brilliant spell at Flamengo won him the 2009 Brazilian title and Player of the Year award, before the lower leagues and retirement. One of football's saddest what-ifs.

## Olof Mellberg
Seven years as Aston Villa's captain made him a Premier League fixture, a no-nonsense Swedish centre-back who threw himself at everything. Mellberg left Villa on a Bosman for Juventus in 2008, then won league titles at Olympiakos before a swansong at Villarreal and MLS side Chivas USA. For Sweden he was an immovable presence across four major tournaments, and at Euro 2012 he scored a brace against England in Kyiv at the age of 34, a reminder that some defenders only get better with age.

## Sami Hyypiä
Liverpool's £2.6m bargain of the century. Houllier signed Hyypiä from Willem II in 1999 and got a decade of commanding, almost serene centre-back play in return. He was central to the 2001 cup treble and lifted the Champions League in Istanbul in 2005, one of the great captain's nights even if Gerrard wore the armband. Bayer Leverkusen gave him a Bundesliga sign-off, and he later managed them. Finland never reached a major tournament with him, which remains a small injustice given how much he carried that defence.

## Walter Samuel
The Wall. Samuel was as hard as the nickname suggested, a no-frills Argentine centre-back who built his reputation at Boca Juniors and then Roma, where he won the 2001 Scudetto. Real Madrid bought him for big money but used him for just one season, and Inter swooped to take him to Milan, where he became the rock of Mourinho's 2010 treble-winning side. He played into his late thirties and won five straight Serie A titles with Inter. Argentina's defensive certainty across a decade, the kind of defender strikers genuinely did not enjoy facing.

## Wayne Bridge
A classy, attacking left-back who came through the Southampton academy before Chelsea bought him in 2003. Bridge won two Premier League titles at Stamford Bridge in the Mourinho years, scoring a famous winner against Arsenal in the 2004 Champions League quarter-final. Manchester City signed him just as the oil money arrived, but he is sadly remembered most for refusing John Terry's handshake at Stamford Bridge in 2010, after the Terry-Vanessa Perroncel story broke. England capped him 36 times, and he loaned out his last seasons across the lower reaches of the league.

## Joleon Lescott
The defender who, as a child, was hit by a car and was told he might never play again. Lescott came through at Wolves, then established himself in the Premier League at Everton under David Moyes, where he was one of the best ball-playing centre-backs in the country. Manchester City paid big money in 2009 and he won two Premier League titles in the early Sheikh Mansour years, including the famous Aguero campaign of 2011-12. West Brom and Aston Villa rounded things off, and 26 England caps tell their own story.

## Thomas Vermaelen
Captain of Arsenal, unused Champions League winner with Barcelona, and a man whose career was haunted by injuries. Vermaelen was outstanding at Ajax before Wenger brought him to north London, where he wore the armband and chipped in with crucial goals from the back. Barcelona was meant to be his crowning move, but he played barely a minute in his first season as they won the treble in 2015. Loans at Roma, a stint at Vissel Kobe alongside Iniesta, and a Belgium career that included third place at the 2018 World Cup added up nicely in the end.

## Yaya Touré
A force of nature. Yaya emerged at Barcelona, where Guardiola memorably played him at centre-back in the 2009 Champions League final, then moved to Manchester City and became one of the Premier League's most devastating midfielders. Three league titles, including the famous Aguero day in 2012 when he was central to the run-in, and 20 league goals in 2013-14. With Ivory Coast he finally lifted the Africa Cup of Nations in 2015 after years of agonising near-misses. African Footballer of the Year four times running. A true one-off.

## Dimitri Payet
A late bloomer who became, for one extraordinary season, the most exciting player in the Premier League. Payet's 2015-16 at West Ham was full of free-kick wonder goals and ridiculous assists, earning him a place in the PFA Team of the Year and a starring role at Euro 2016 on home soil, where he scored against Romania in the opener. Twice a Marseille player, he had two great spells at the Velodrome either side of the West Ham fairy-tale. His exit from London, where he downed tools to force a return to France, soured the legacy but cannot dim the football.

## Wilfried Bony
A short, blunt-force striker who at his peak was unplayable. Bony scored 31 goals in his second season at Vitesse, then arrived at Swansea and proved it was no fluke, finishing as the Premier League's top scorer across the 2014 calendar year. Manchester City bought him in January 2015 after he had won the Africa Cup of Nations with Ivory Coast, and he came on to lift the League Cup. The City move never really worked, and loans and a return to Swansea followed, but the Premier League at its peak saw the real Bony.

## Bojan Krkić
Barcelona's youngest ever league scorer at 17, anointed as the next Messi before he had even started shaving. The pressure broke him. Bojan struggled with anxiety attacks and famously pulled himself out of Spain's Euro 2008 squad. He drifted on loan to Roma and Milan, then found something like peace at Stoke City, where he became a beloved cult hero in the Mark Hughes era before a serious knee injury derailed things. Mainz, Montreal and a coaching return to Barca rounded out a career that quietly carried more weight than most.

## Aaron Lennon
A blur of pace on the right wing, picked up from Leeds' youth ranks. Lennon spent a decade at Tottenham, a key part of Harry Redknapp's Champions League side and the Bale-era teams, with 21 England caps along the way. Everton and Burnley provided steady late-career years, but the most important thing he did happened off the pitch. Detained under the Mental Health Act in 2017, he came back and became one of football's most articulate voices on mental health. Brave in a way the position never demanded.

## Andros Townsend
A flying right winger and one of the great loan stories of his generation. Townsend went out nine times from Tottenham before he started a Premier League game, learning the trade at Yeovil, Millwall, Watford and Birmingham. He scored a thunderbolt on his England debut against Montenegro in 2013 at Wembley, then drifted from Spurs to Newcastle, Palace and Everton, where his Premier League goal-of-the-season volley against Manchester City in 2018-19 was the highlight. A serious knee injury at Goodison effectively closed the door before a Luton swansong.

## Florent Malouda
Born in French Guiana, raised on flair. Malouda became the heart of an outstanding Lyon side that won four straight Ligue 1 titles in the mid-2000s, then moved to Chelsea in 2007 and won the Premier League, the FA Cup and, in 2012, the Champions League in Munich. He scored in the final against Bayern. Spells in Turkey, Egypt, Belgium and India saw him chase the football around the world deep into his thirties, and France capped him 80 times, with a runners-up medal from the 2006 World Cup as a teammate of Zidane and co.

## David Villa
Spain's all-time top scorer. El Guaje rose at Sporting Gijon, exploded at Valencia, then moved to Barcelona where he won La Liga, the Champions League and just about everything else under Guardiola. He scored five goals at the 2010 World Cup as Spain were crowned champions, and he had already won Euro 2008. Atletico Madrid gave him a famous final La Liga title in 2014 as Simeone's outsiders broke the duopoly. He finished up in MLS with NYCFC and at Vissel Kobe in Japan, scoring everywhere he went. A pure, ruthless centre-forward.

## Clarence Seedorf
The only player to win the Champions League with three different clubs, and it might as well have been four. Seedorf lifted the trophy with Ajax in 1995, Real Madrid in 1998, and Milan in 2003 and 2007, a record that will probably never be broken. Add an Inter chapter and you have a man who played for four of European football's biggest names. A cultured, powerful midfielder with a piledriver of a long-range shot, he was a Dutch institution for nearly two decades. The trophies tell you everything.
## Michael Ballack
The midfielder who collected runners-up medals like other players collected winners' ones. Michael Ballack came of age scoring the own goal that helped Kaiserslautern to a shock 1998 Bundesliga title, then became the heartbeat of Bayer Leverkusen's heartbreak 2002 side that lost the league, cup and Champions League final inside a fortnight. At Bayern he finally won three doubles, and a free transfer to Chelsea brought Premier League titles and FA Cups. Kevin-Prince Boateng's tackle in 2010 robbed him of a final World Cup, the cruel coda to a brilliant, near-miss career.

## Fabien Barthez
The bald, charismatic Frenchman who won a Champions League at 21 with Marseille and never quite stopped being unpredictable. Fabien Barthez moved to Monaco, then Manchester United for a world-record fee for a goalkeeper, where his sweeping and ball-playing dazzled before the howlers crept in. He returned to France with Nantes, lifted the 1998 World Cup with Zinedine Zidane kissing his head for luck, and added Euro 2000 for good measure. Retirement brought a second career in motor racing, because of course it did.

## Ruud van Nistelrooy
A born finisher who started life as a defender, suffered a cruciate injury on the eve of his Manchester United move, came back and tore the Premier League apart. Ruud van Nistelrooy banged in 150 goals at Old Trafford, then won the Pichichi twice at Real Madrid before winding down at Málaga and Hamburg. PSV had launched him; Eindhoven welcomed him home. In management he has cut a steady figure with Burnley, a Manchester United caretaker stint and Leicester, the penalty-box predator turned thoughtful coach.

## Tomáš Rosický
Little Mozart. Tomáš Rosický conducted Sparta Prague as a teenager, won the 2002 Bundesliga with Dortmund, and then spent a decade at Arsenal where his vision, scampering runs and Theatre of Dreams strike against Spurs lit up the rare seasons his hamstrings allowed. Injuries cost him whole years of his prime, but he never lost the elegance or the smile. He returned to Sparta to finish where he began, and remains the most-capped Czech outfield player of the modern era.

## Henrik Larsson
From stacking fruit in a Helsingborg warehouse to seven seasons of unrelenting goals at Celtic, 242 of them, Henrik Larsson built one of the great underrated careers. Feyenoord launched him; the Hoops adored him. A move to Barcelona at 32 brought a Champions League winner's medal in 2006, and the famous Thierry Henry line that "people speak about Ronaldinho and Eto'o but I think Larsson won the final". A loan stint at Manchester United enchanted Old Trafford, and he ended back at Helsingborg, eventually playing alongside his son Jordan.

## Jamie Carragher
A Bootle-born Evertonian who became one of Anfield's most decorated lifers. Jamie Carragher made over 700 appearances for Liverpool, all heart and positional intelligence, never quite quick but always in the right place. He was the cramping, defiant figure who refused to lose in Istanbul 2005, dragging Liverpool through a Champions League final that should not have been possible. FA Cups, League Cups and a UEFA Cup followed. Since hanging up the boots he has become Sky's sharpest analyst, the scouse half of the country's most quoted punditry double act.

## Ledley King
The defender who could barely train, and was still better than nearly everyone who could. Ledley King's knee cartilage was worn through by his mid-twenties, leaving him to play matches off little more than physio and adrenaline, often turning up only on Fridays. At Tottenham, where he spent his entire career, his reading of the game was so good that Thierry Henry called him the toughest defender he ever faced. He lifted the 2008 League Cup, his only major trophy, and remains the great Spurs what-if.

## Yossi Benayoun
Israel's most gifted modern footballer, a wandering playmaker who turned up everywhere. Yossi Benayoun left Hapoel Be'er Sheva for the Ajax youth academy before settling at Racing Santander, then dazzled in England with West Ham, Liverpool, Chelsea, an Arsenal loan and finally QPR. He won a Champions League winner's medal at Stamford Bridge and a Europa League at Chelsea, while captaining Israel to 102 caps. A footballer's footballer with the softest of touches and one of the kindest reputations in the dressing room.

## Owen Hargreaves
Calgary-born, Bayern-raised, England's most unusual midfielder of the 2000s. Owen Hargreaves came through at Bayern Munich, winning a Champions League at 19, and was arguably England's best player at the 2006 World Cup playing out of position at right-back. A blockbuster move to Manchester United brought a free-kick goal and a winners' medal in Moscow as a substitute in the 2008 final, before chronic tendinitis robbed him of his late twenties. A Manchester City free transfer offered one last cameo. A career of brilliance bookended by what might have been.

## Jermain Defoe
A poacher's poacher who refused to fade away. Jermain Defoe burst out of West Ham, infamously biting Javier Mascherano along the way, then scored goals everywhere he went: two spells at Tottenham, Portsmouth, a Sunderland relegation fight that he very nearly won single-handed, Toronto, Bournemouth, and finally Rangers in the SPL. He finished with 162 Premier League goals. Above all that statistical noise sits his friendship with young Sunderland fan Bradley Lowery, an act of footballing love that endeared him to the country far beyond his goals.

## Darren Bent
An English centre-forward who was steadily, unfussily excellent for the best part of a decade. Darren Bent came through Ipswich, banged them in for Charlton, then traded up to Tottenham, Sunderland (for a then-British record from Wearside), Aston Villa, Fulham, Brighton and Derby. He scored 106 Premier League goals despite playing for almost every mid-table side. He will, inevitably, always be the man whose deflected shot off a beach ball beat Pepe Reina at the Stadium of Light in 2009, the most ridiculous goal of the Premier League era.

## Andrey Arshavin
The cheeky, broad-shouldered Russian who briefly lit up the world. Andrey Arshavin won the UEFA Cup with Zenit Saint Petersburg in 2008, then danced through Euro 2008 as Russia reached the semi-finals, drawing every big club in Europe. Arsenal got him for a club-record fee and, on a wild April night at Anfield, he scored four against Liverpool in a 4-4 draw. The brilliance was sporadic after that. He drifted back to Zenit, then Kuban, and finally Kairat in Kazakhstan, the smile never quite leaving him.

## Heurelho Gomes
Brazilian, eccentric, beloved. Heurelho Gomes won three Eredivisie titles in goal for PSV before a high-profile move to Tottenham where his shot-stopping was elite and his handling was, charitably, an adventure. Spurs fans never quite forgave him; Watford fans absolutely did. He became a Vicarage Road folk hero across nearly a decade, lifting the Hornets through promotion, an FA Cup final and relegation alike. He briefly retired in 2020, only to come back later the same week when Watford realised they still needed him. Few keepers have been more loved.

## Jonathan Woodgate
An England centre-back of genuine class whose body would not cooperate. Jonathan Woodgate came through the Leeds academy in their Champions League years, moved to Newcastle and earned a glamour transfer to Real Madrid, where he marked his debut by scoring an own goal and being sent off. Injuries dogged him at Middlesbrough and then Tottenham, where he did head home the winning goal in the 2008 League Cup final. He later managed Boro and coached at Bournemouth, calmer in the dugout than his career ever allowed him to be on the pitch.

## Michael Carrick
The metronome. Michael Carrick was one of West Ham's four amigos, did a quiet, classy season at Tottenham, and then spent 12 years at Manchester United changing tempo with a single pass. Five Premier League titles, a Champions League in Moscow 2008, a Europa League and just about everything else followed, yet he won only 34 England caps, a national oversight. Sir Alex Ferguson once said he was as good a passer as Paul Scholes. He returned to Old Trafford as a coach, briefly stepped up as head coach, and is now managing in his own right.

## Damien Duff
A scruffy, hard-running Irish winger with a low centre of gravity and a knack for the big moment. Damien Duff was the heartbeat of the Blackburn side that survived and then lifted the 2002 League Cup, before Chelsea paid £17m and he won back-to-back Premier Leagues under Jose Mourinho. Newcastle followed, then Fulham, where he scored in the 2010 Europa League final. He saw out his playing days in Melbourne and at Shamrock Rovers, before returning to manage the Hoops to back-to-back League of Ireland titles.

## Stewart Downing
A proper old-school English left winger whose career felt like a barometer of the early Premier League boom. Stewart Downing came through at Middlesbrough, helped them to a UEFA Cup final in 2006, and earned a big move to Aston Villa before Liverpool spent £20m on him in 2011, a deal that defined the post-Torres anxiety on Merseyside. He rebuilt at West Ham, looked like a genuinely top-class creator again, and finished by helping Boro back to the Premier League. Quiet, technically sound, and frequently underrated.

## Yohan Cabaye
A snappy, neat French midfielder who always seemed to play for a club punching above its weight. Yohan Cabaye won Ligue 1 and the Coupe de France in Lille's brilliant 2011 double, then moved to Newcastle, where his goals and passing carried a team that finished fifth. PSG bought him back to France, Crystal Palace took him to the FA Cup final, and Saint-Étienne gave him a final ride. He earned 48 France caps across Euro 2012 and the 2014 World Cup, the dependable engine no Didier Deschamps midfield was without.

## Mark Viduka
Big, brilliant and beloved. Mark Viduka left Dinamo Zagreb for Celtic, where he walked out on the club one game in citing depression, returned and started scoring for fun, then signed for Leeds in their Champions League pomp. He scored all four in a 4-3 win over Liverpool that fans still bore each other with. Middlesbrough got the best of him next, helped him to a UEFA Cup final, and Newcastle had the last chapter. He captained Australia at the 2006 World Cup, the most technically gifted Socceroos striker of his generation.

## Esteban Cambiasso
A bald, anticipatory midfielder who made winning look like an administrative task. Esteban Cambiasso came through River Plate, won La Liga at Real Madrid while spending much of it on the bench, then moved to Inter and won five straight Scudetti, ending with Jose Mourinho's 2010 treble. Released at 34, he turned up at Leicester and was voted their player of the season in the 2014-15 great escape, the campaign before the title. He finished at Olympiakos, then went into coaching, the kind of player every dressing room wishes it had.

## Marco Reus
The Borussia Dortmund icon who suffered for his loyalty. Marco Reus came home from Mönchengladbach in 2012 and stayed for 12 years, scoring relentlessly when fit, never quite winning the Bundesliga, watching teammate after teammate leave for Bavaria. A pre-tournament injury ruled him out of Germany's 2014 World Cup-winning squad in the cruellest possible timing. Two German Footballer of the Year awards say what trophies could not. He left Dortmund in 2024 for LA Galaxy, the cult hero finally allowed a curtain call abroad.

## Aaron Ramsey
The Cardiff academy product who became Arsenal's big-game man. Aaron Ramsey spent a decade in north London, winning two FA Cups and scoring the winning goal in both finals, the second one a sumptuous backheel. He survived a horror leg break at Stoke in 2010 to come back better. Juventus took him on a free, then Rangers loaned him in for a wild SPL season, Nice gave him a French stint, and a homecoming move to Cardiff and a spell at Pumas UNAM followed. He captained Wales to the 2016 Euros semi-finals, the country's best modern tournament.

## Daley Blind
Footballing brain, footballing surname. Daley Blind played anywhere Ajax or Manchester United needed him: left-back, centre-back, defensive midfield, all with the same calm distribution learned from his father Danny. He won the Eredivisie multiple times at Ajax, played a key role in the 2014 World Cup semi-final run, and lifted the 2017 Europa League at Old Trafford. A heart condition diagnosed in 2019 ended for many but not him; he carried on, returning to Ajax to win another title, then taking a Bayern Munich loan and a stint at Girona.

## Lucas Moura
A Brazilian winger who left a hat-trick in football's collective memory. Lucas Moura broke through at São Paulo, became Paris Saint-Germain's club-record signing in 2013, and then moved to Tottenham, where he spent five mostly tidy years. Then came Amsterdam, 2019, Champions League semi-final, three goals in the second half against Ajax to send Spurs to the final on away goals, including the last-second winner. He returned to São Paulo to finish at the club that built him, but those 45 minutes are immortal.

## Andy Carroll
A long-haired, headband-wearing throwback, the most expensive British footballer in history for about a fortnight. Andy Carroll came through Newcastle and forced a £35m move to Liverpool in January 2011, where the goals never quite flowed. West Ham, first on loan then permanently, became his home; he then drifted via Newcastle again, Reading and Bordeaux. He scored a brilliant header against Sweden at Euro 2012 and made the world remember exactly what English centre-forwards used to do. Injuries took the rest.

## Memphis Depay
A swaggering, tattooed Dutch forward who has had at least three careers. Memphis Depay won the Eredivisie Golden Boot at PSV in 2014-15, flopped at Manchester United at 21, and rebuilt himself into a complete attacker at Lyon. Barcelona snapped him up on a free, Atlético Madrid followed, and a move to Corinthians took him to Brazil. Along the way he became the Netherlands' all-time leading scorer in 2025, passing Robin van Persie, the swagger justified at last. Quiet on the pitch, very much not off it.
